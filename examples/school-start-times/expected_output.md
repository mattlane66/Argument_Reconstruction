# Expected Output: School Start Times

## Charitable reconstruction
They are arguing that the school should start later because the current start time leaves students too tired to function well in first period, and a later start may improve sleep, attention, learning, mental health, and safety.

## Conclusion
They are trying to prove that the school should start later.

## Explicit premises
1. Students are tired in first period.
2. Teen sleep biology makes early starts especially hard.
3. Later start times may increase sleep.
4. More sleep may improve attention and learning.
5. More sleep may improve mental health.
6. More sleep may reduce teen driving accidents.

## Hidden bridge
For that to follow, you would also have to assume that first-period tiredness is caused mainly by the school start time, that the tiredness is significant and widespread, that a later start would meaningfully improve sleep, and that the benefits would outweigh tradeoffs such as transportation, childcare, work schedules, after-school activities, and family routines.

## Linkage
Mixed. The core causal chain is serial: later start -> more sleep -> better attention, learning, health, and safety. The final policy case is also convergent because learning, mental health, and driving safety are separate reasons that can each support the conclusion.

## Removal test
If the tiredness premise is removed, the argument loses its motivating problem. If the sleep-improvement premise is removed, most of the causal chain breaks. If one benefit is removed, such as driving safety, the other benefits may still support the policy.

## Counterexample
A district might shift school later and slightly increase sleep, but create larger harms through bus scheduling, childcare conflicts, after-school work disruption, or later extracurriculars. In that case, the premises could be partly true while the conclusion that the school should start later is still false or under-supported.

## Shortcut / fallacy check
The weak version risks sliding from "some students are tired" to "the whole schedule should change." The stronger version repairs this by requiring evidence that tiredness is widespread, schedule-caused, materially harmful, and better solved by later starts than by alternatives.

## Reality check
This is a practical policy argument, so the standard is cogency rather than deductive validity. The argument becomes stronger if the causal premises are empirically supported and if the benefits outweigh implementation costs.

## Better version of the argument
School should start later if evidence shows that current start times materially reduce teen sleep, that later starts reliably increase sleep and improve important outcomes, and that the gains outweigh operational and family tradeoffs.

## What would change my mind
- Evidence that later starts do not meaningfully increase sleep.
- Evidence that benefits are small relative to costs.
- Evidence that a narrower intervention solves the problem better.
